If vector a goes 3 up and 2 over, and b goes 1 up and 3 over, then vector a + b goes up 4 (3 + 1 up) and 5 over (2 + 3 over). It's worth noting that vectors or matrices that are not of the same size cannot be added together! This means you cannot add a 4x2 matrix to a 5x3 matrix. This also applies to vectors.
Steps:
Add the first element in both matrices, then the second, third, and so on...
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} a&b&c\\ d&e&f\\ g&h&i \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} j&k&l\\ m&n&o\\ p&q&r \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a+j&b+k&c+l\\ d+m&e+n&f+o\\ g+p&h+q&i+r \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Examples:
1) Let's start out by adding two vectors. Just add each element in the same row:
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 9\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 9\\ 7\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1+9\\ 9+7\\ 2+3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10\\ 16\\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
And that is the sum of the vectors.

2) Suppose we have our 3x3 matrices. We add each corresponding element:
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1&5&0\\ 6&2&8\\ 1&3&4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 9&0&8\\ 3&1&7\\ 5&3&4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1+9&5+0&0+8\\ 6+3&2+1&0+7\\ 1+5&3+3&4+4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10&5&8\\ 9&6&7\\ 6&6&8 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Remember that this only works since we are trying to add two matrices that have the same size! This is different in matrix multiplication, where you might multiply a 3x2 matrix by a 2x3 matrix.

3) Let's try another one, but this time with different dimensions:
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 12&3&16\\ 1&19&32 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 14&60&5\\ 42&7&19 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12+14&3+60&16+5\\ 1+42&19+7&32+19 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 26&63&21\\ 43&26&51 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Simple as that!
